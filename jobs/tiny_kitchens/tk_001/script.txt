Pull out your grandmother's cast iron skillet, and it's smoother than the shiny new one in the store. How does an old pan beat a brand-new one?

The secret is the black coating, called seasoning. It's not dirt — it's oil that's been transformed by heat.

Here's the magic. When you heat oil past about 350 degrees, the fat molecules break apart and link together into a tough new layer called polymer.

Every time you cook, a wafer-thin film bonds onto the iron. One coat is fragile. But fifty dinners later, those layers stack into a slick, glassy shell.

That shell fills the pan's microscopic pores, so eggs glide right off — no chemical coating required.

And the surprise? That non-stick layer is technically plastic — the same kind of chemistry that makes paint and varnish, grown one breakfast at a time.

So an old skillet isn't worn out. It's been quietly building armor, meal after meal, for decades.
