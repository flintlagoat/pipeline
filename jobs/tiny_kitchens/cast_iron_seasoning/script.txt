Crack an egg into a brand-new cast iron skillet and it welds itself to the metal like it's trying to escape. Crack the same egg into your grandmother's beat-up black skillet, and it slides around like the pan is buttered. Same iron. Same egg. The difference is a layer of oil that has literally turned into plastic.

That's the part nobody tells you. The slickness everyone calls "seasoning" isn't grease, and it isn't magic. It's a hard, smooth coating you build out of cooked oil — and the chemistry behind it is the same chemistry as drying paint. Stick around, because once you see what's happening to that oil, you'll never wipe out your pan the same way again.

But first, the obvious question. Why does a fresh-from-the-factory pan stick so badly? Look at raw cast iron under magnification and it's a moonscape — pits, ridges, microscopic canyons. Egg proteins flow into those canyons and, when they hit hot metal, form actual chemical bonds with the iron. Your egg isn't resting on the pan. It's gripping it.

So an old pan should be worse, right? More scratched, more abused, decades of cooking. Therefore rougher, therefore stickier. Except it's the opposite. And to understand why, we have to go back to the single thing that started cast iron's whole story: it rusts.

Iron and oxygen are in love. Leave bare cast iron damp and it turns orange in a day. So before the 1950s, when these pans were in nearly every American kitchen, people needed a barrier between the iron and the air. The cheapest barrier around was fat. You wiped the pan with lard, you put it near the fire, and you went on with your life.

But that thin film of fat, sitting on hot metal in the presence of air, doesn't just protect the iron. It starts to change.

Cooking oils are made of long molecules called triglycerides — picture three floppy carbon chains tied together at one end. The good seasoning oils, like flaxseed or old-recipe lard, are loaded with unsaturated chains, meaning the carbon backbone has weak spots: double bonds. Heat plus oxygen attacks those weak spots and snaps them open, leaving each molecule with a reactive, grabby end.

And a molecule with a grabby end goes looking for another molecule to grab. They link. Then those links link. One floppy oil molecule joins to the next, until thousands have stitched together into one enormous, continuous web. That web stops being a liquid. It becomes a solid film bonded to the metal.

That process has a name: polymerization. It's the exact same reaction that turns linseed oil into the hard finish on a wooden table, and why old oil paint dries rock-hard instead of staying wet. Your seasoning is, chemically, a coat of varnish — one you cooked on yourself, one molecule at a time.

So picture what's happening every single time you heat that pan. You wipe on a whisper-thin layer of oil. The heat polymerizes it into a glassy sheet maybe a few molecules thick. Next cook, another sheet forms on top — and crucially, it fills in a little more of those microscopic canyons. The pan isn't getting rougher with age. It's getting smoother — the valleys slowly paving themselves flat with hardened oil-plastic.

This is why your grandmother's skillet beats a $200 new one. It isn't sentiment. She has dozens of polymerized layers built up over decades, sanding the surface down toward a mirror. A new pan has zero. You can buy expensive iron, but you cannot buy time on the stove.

Now — the soap thing. You've heard it your whole life: never, ever use soap on cast iron, you'll destroy the seasoning. Whole families guard this rule like scripture. And it's wrong.

Remember what the seasoning actually is. It's not oil sitting on the surface, where soap could lift it away. It's a polymer — a hardened plastic web chemically locked to the iron. Dish soap is built to dissolve loose, liquid grease. It has no power over a cured solid, any more than washing your dinner plate strips the glaze off the ceramic. Scrub your seasoned pan with soap and a sponge and the coating doesn't even notice.

So where did the fear come from? Old soaps, decades ago, contained lye — strong enough to slowly chew through the polymer. That hasn't been true of kitchen dish soap for generations. The warning outlived the reason. What actually wrecks seasoning is steel wool stripping it off mechanically, or letting the pan sit wet so rust creeps up underneath and lifts the coating from below.

And here's the payoff the whole thing has been building toward. We treat "seasoning" like a vague folk practice — a thing you do and hope. But it's a genuine engineered surface, governed by real, repeatable chemistry. The crackly black layer on a hundred-year-old skillet is a true plastic polymer, bonded to iron, polished molecule by molecule by every meal that pan has ever made. It is the only nonstick surface on Earth that your cooking actively repairs.

Think about what that means. A Teflon pan is best on day one and degrades from there — every scratch is permanent loss. Cast iron is the inverse. The damage you'd expect from many fried eggs is exactly the process that makes it better.

Which is why that single, almost careless decision — just rub it with fat so it won't rust — turned out to be one of the most quietly brilliant pieces of accidental engineering in any kitchen. Nobody set out to invent a self-healing polymer over a fire. They were only trying to keep the iron from going orange.

So the next time an egg glides across that black surface, remember you're not cooking on metal at all. You're cooking on cooked oil that became plastic. And if oil can turn solid just from heat and air... what do you think is happening inside that bottle of olive oil slowly going rancid in your cupboard?
