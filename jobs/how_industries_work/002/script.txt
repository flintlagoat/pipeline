Your gym sold roughly ten times more memberships than it can physically hold. A standard club fits a few hundred bodies. It signed up six thousand people. And the entire business model depends on one thing — you breaking your New Year's resolution by February.

Walk onto the floor of a mid-size gym. Maybe forty treadmills. A few dozen machines. Some free weights. Do the math on a fire-code basis and the room legally holds somewhere around three hundred people at once. Now look at the membership database. Planet Fitness clubs average over six thousand members per location. Some carry more than ten thousand.

If even a fraction showed up, the place would be a riot. So why isn't it? Why can you walk in on a Tuesday evening and find an open treadmill? Because they're counting on you not coming. And the reason that works is stranger than the empty floor itself.

Here's the first gear. The average gym member visits a little over twice a month — while paying for unlimited access. You're not buying gym time. You're buying the feeling that you might go. And gyms learned that feeling sells far better than the gym itself.

Now the second gear, and this is where it stops being an accident. Roughly half of new members stop showing up within the first six months. A huge chunk vanish by week five. The gym knows this curve cold. So they don't just tolerate the dropouts — they build the company around them.

Think about what a ghost member actually is. Someone paying twenty, thirty, forty dollars a month for a service that costs the gym nothing to deliver — because they never walk in. No towel to wash. No machine wear. No staff time. That dues payment is almost pure profit. The industry even has a quiet name for them: the "sleeping" members. And a typical club's profit doesn't come from the people sweating on the floor. It comes from the ones who forgot they ever joined.

Which explains the strangest design choice in the whole business. Why is it so absurdly easy to sign up and so brutally hard to quit?

You can join in ninety seconds on your phone. Cancelling? You might need to mail a physical letter. Or show up in person during specific hours. Or call a number that's only staffed part-time. For years some chains required a notarized cancellation request — an actual notary stamp to stop paying for a treadmill. This isn't incompetence. It's friction, engineered on purpose, because every extra hoop buys one more billing cycle from a member who's already mentally gone.

The Federal Trade Commission noticed. In 2023 it proposed a "click to cancel" rule — quitting should be as easy as joining. The industry's response tells you everything. They lobbied hard against it. If the ghost members were a rounding error, nobody fights to keep the maze.

Now look at January with new eyes. The treadmill commercials. The "no commitment" deals. The dollar-down promotions. Those ads aren't hunting for athletes. They're hunting for the exact person statistically most likely to quit by March — the motivated, guilty, optimistic newcomer who buys a year and uses it for five weeks. That person pays like a regular and consumes like a ghost. The most profitable customer a gym can find.

And the pricing locks the trap shut. A month-to-month plan looks expensive next to the annual contract. So people choose the annual deal, telling themselves it'll force commitment. It does the opposite. It guarantees twelve months of dues from someone who'll attend for two — and removes the easy monthly off-ramp right when motivation collapses.

So here's the held-back number that proves the whole machine. A club built for three hundred sells six thousand memberships — a twenty-to-one oversell. It survives because only about one in ten members visits in any given week. And its margin lives almost entirely inside the half who quit but keep paying. The gym you picture as a place for fitness is, mathematically, a subscription business that happens to own some equipment. The treadmills are set dressing. The product is the lapse.

Which is why the friendliest face at the front desk on January second isn't rooting for your transformation. They're rooting for your optimism — and quietly betting against your follow-through.

And once you see a business that profits from customers who never show up, you start spotting it everywhere. The meditation app you downloaded once. The streaming service for the channel you never watch. The storage unit holding boxes you'll never open. So which industry has perfected the art of selling you a thing they're praying you forget to use? That's the next door worth opening.
