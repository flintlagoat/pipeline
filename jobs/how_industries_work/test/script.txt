You buy a coffee for three dollars. Simple, right? There's a hidden ladder behind that cup that decides who actually keeps the money.

Start with the bean. A farmer in Ethiopia gets paid around 7 cents for the coffee inside your cup. Seven cents.

Then it moves. An exporter, a shipping container, an importer, a roaster — each one adds a markup before it reaches the café's back door.

By the time it's a green sack in the roaster's warehouse, the cost is maybe 30 cents. Roasting and packaging push it past a dollar.

Now the café. Rent, the espresso machine, the barista's wage, the paper cup — these eat most of the three dollars, not the coffee itself.

Here's the reveal. The cheapest ingredient in your latte is the actual coffee. You're paying for everything around it.

That cup was never about the bean. It was about the building.
